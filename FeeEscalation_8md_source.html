8 ## Fee Escalation
9 
10 The guiding principal of fee escalation is that when things are going
11 smoothly, fees stay low, but as soon as high levels of traffic appear
12 on the network, fees will grow quickly to extreme levels. This should
13 dissuade malicious users from abusing the system, while giving
14 legitimate users the ability to pay a higher fee to get high-priority
15 transactions into the open ledger, even during unfavorable conditions.
16 
17 How fees escalate:
18 
19 1. There is a base [fee level](#fee-level) of 256,
20 which is the minimum that a typical transaction
21 is required to pay. For a [reference
22 transaction](#reference-transaction), that corresponds to the
23 network base fee, which is currently 10 drops.
24 2. However, there is a limit on the number of transactions that
25 can get into an open ledger for that base fee level. The limit
26 will vary based on the [health](#consensus-health) of the
27 consensus process, but will be at least [5](#other-constants).
28  * If consensus stays [healthy](#consensus-health), the limit will
29  be the max of the number of transactions in the validated ledger
30  plus [20%](#other-constants) or the current limit until it gets
31  to [50](#other-constants), at which point, the limit will be the
32  largest number of transactions plus [20%](#other-constants)
33  in the last [20](#other-constants) validated ledgers which had
34  more than [50](#other-constants) transactions. Any time the limit
35  decreases (i.e. a large ledger is no longer recent), the limit will
36  decrease to the new largest value by 10% each time the ledger has
37  more than 50 transactions.
38  * If consensus does not stay [healthy](#consensus-health),
39  the limit will clamp down to the smaller of the number of
40  transactions in the validated ledger minus [50%](#other-constants)
41  or the previous limit minus [50%](#other-constants).
42  * The intended effect of these mechanisms is to allow as many base fee
43  level transactions to get into the ledger as possible while the
44  network is [healthy](#consensus-health), but to respond quickly to
45  any condition that makes it [unhealthy](#consensus-health), including,
46  but not limited to, malicious attacks.
47 3. Once there are more transactions in the open ledger than indicated
48 by the limit, the required fee level jumps drastically.
49  * The formula is `( lastLedgerMedianFeeLevel *
50  TransactionsInOpenLedger^2 / limit^2 )`,
51  and returns a [fee level](#fee-level).
52 4. That may still be pretty small, but as more transactions get
53 into the ledger, the fee level increases exponentially.
54  * For example, if the limit is 6, and the median fee is minimal,
55  and assuming all [reference transactions](#reference-transaction),
56  the 8th transaction only requires a [level](#fee-level) of about 174,000
57  or about 6800 drops,
58  but the 20th transaction requires a [level](#fee-level) of about
59  1,283,000 or about 50,000 drops.
60 5. Finally, as each ledger closes, the median fee level of that ledger is
61 computed and used as `lastLedgerMedianFeeLevel` (with a
62 [minimum value of 128,000](#other-constants))
63 in the fee escalation formula for the next open ledger.
64  * Continuing the example above, if ledger consensus completes with
65  only those 20 transactions, and all of those transactions paid the
66  minimum required fee at each step, the limit will be adjusted from
67  6 to 24, and the `lastLedgerMedianFeeLevel` will be about 322,000,
68  which is 12,600 drops for a
69  [reference transaction](#reference-transaction).
70  * This will only require 10 drops for the first 25 transactions,
71  but the 26th transaction will require a level of about 349,150
72  or about 13,649 drops.
73 
74 * This example assumes a cold-start scenario, with a single, possibly
75 malicious, user willing to pay arbitrary amounts to get transactions
76 into the open ledger. It ignores the effects of the [Transaction
77 Queue](#transaction-queue). Any lower fee level transactions submitted
78 by other users at the same time as this user's transactions will go into
79 the transaction queue, and will have the first opportunity to be applied
80 to the _next_ open ledger. The next section describes how that works in
81 more detail.

83 ## Transaction Queue
84 
85 An integral part of making fee escalation work for users of the network
86 is the transaction queue. The queue allows legitimate transactions to be
87 considered by the network for future ledgers if the escalated open
88 ledger fee gets too high. This allows users to submit low priority
89 transactions with a low fee, and wait for high fees to drop. It also
90 allows legitimate users to continue submitting transactions during high
91 traffic periods, and give those transactions a much better chance to
92 succeed.
93 
94 1. If an incoming transaction meets both the base [fee
95 level](#fee-level) and the load fee minimum, but does not have a high
96 enough [fee level](#fee-level) to immediately go into the open ledger,
97 it is instead put into the queue and broadcast to peers. Each peer will
98 then make an independent decision about whether to put the transaction
99 into its open ledger or the queue. In principle, peers with identical
100 open ledgers will come to identical decisions. Any discrepancies will be
101 resolved as usual during consensus.
102 2. When consensus completes, the open ledger limit is adjusted, and
103 the required [fee level](#fee-level) drops back to the base
104 [fee level](#fee-level). Before the ledger is made available to
105 external transactions, transactions are applied from the queue to the
106 ledger from highest [fee level](#fee-level) to lowest. These transactions
107 count against the open ledger limit, so the required [fee level](#fee-level)
108 may start rising during this process.
109 3. Once the queue is empty, or the required [fee level](#fee-level)
110 rises too high for the remaining transactions in the queue, the ledger
111 is opened up for normal transaction processing.
112 4. A transaction in the queue can stay there indefinitely in principle,
113 but in practice, either
114  * it will eventually get applied to the ledger,
115  * it will attempt to apply to the ledger and fail,
116  * it will attempt to apply to the ledger and retry [10
117  times](#other-constants),
118  * its last ledger sequence number will expire,
119  * the user will replace it by submitting another transaction with the same
120  sequence number and at least a [25% higher fee](#other-constants), or
121  * it will get dropped when the queue fills up with more valuable transactions.
122  The size limit is computed dynamically, and can hold transactions for
123  the next [20 ledgers](#other-constants) (restricted to a minimum of
124  [2000 transactions](#other-constants)). The lower the transaction's
125  fee, the more likely that it will get dropped if the network is busy.
126 
127 If a transaction is submitted for an account with one or more transactions
128 already in the queue, and a sequence number that is sequential with the other
129 transactions in the queue for that account, it will be considered
130 for the queue if it meets these additional criteria:
131  * the account has fewer than [10](#other-constants) transactions
132  already in the queue.
133  * it pays a [fee level](#fee-level) that is greater than 10% of the
134  fee level for the transaction with the previous sequence number,
135  * all other queued transactions for that account, in the case where
136  they spend the maximum possible XRP, leave enough XRP balance to pay
137  the fee,
138  * the total fees for the other queued transactions are less than both
139  the network's minimum reserve and the account's XRP balance, and
140  * none of the prior queued transactions affect the ability of subsequent
141  transactions to claim a fee.
142 
143 Currently, there is an additional restriction that the queue cannot work with
144 transactions using the `sfPreviousTxnID` or `sfAccountTxnID` fields.
145 `sfPreviousTxnID` is deprecated and shouldn't be used anyway. Future
146 development will make the queue aware of `sfAccountTxnID` mechanisms.

148 ## Technical Details
149 
150 ### Fee Level
151 
152 "Fee level" is used to allow the cost of different types of transactions
153 to be compared directly. For a [reference
154 transaction](#reference-transaction), the base fee
155 level is 256. If a transaction is submitted with a higher `Fee` field,
156 the fee level is scaled appropriately.
157 
158 Examples, assuming a [reference transaction](#reference-transaction)
159 base fee of 10 drops:
160 
161 1. A single-signed [reference transaction](#reference-transaction)
162 with `Fee=20` will have a fee level of
163 `20 drop fee * 256 fee level / 10 drop base fee = 512 fee level`.
164 2. A multi-signed [reference transaction](#reference-transaction) with
165 3 signatures (base fee = 40 drops) and `Fee=60` will have a fee level of
166 `60 drop fee * 256 fee level / ((1tx + 3sigs) * 10 drop base fee) = 384
167 fee level`.
168 3. A hypothetical future non-reference transaction with a base
169 fee of 15 drops multi-signed with 5 signatures and `Fee=90` will
170 have a fee level of
171 `90 drop fee * 256 fee level / ((1tx + 5sigs) * 15 drop base fee) = 256
172 fee level`.
173 
174 This demonstrates that a simpler transaction paying less XRP can be more
175 likely to get into the open ledger, or be sorted earlier in the queue
176 than a more complex transaction paying more XRP.
177 
178 ### Reference Transaction
179 
180 In this document, a "Reference Transaction" is any currently implemented
181 single-signed transaction (eg. Payment, Account Set, Offer Create, etc)
182 that requires a fee.
183 
184 In the future, there may be other transaction types that require
185 more (or less) work for rippled to process. Those transactions may have
186 a higher (or lower) base fee, requiring a correspondingly higher (or
187 lower) fee to get into the same position as a reference transaction.
188 
189 ### Consensus Health
190 
191 For consensus to be considered healthy, the consensus process must take
192 less than 5 seconds. This time limit was chosen based on observed past
193 behavior of the ripple network. Note that this is not necessarily the
194 time between ledger closings, as consensus usually starts some amount
195 of time after a ledger opens.
196 
197 ### Other Constants
198 
199 * *Base fee transaction limit per ledger*. The minimum value of 5 was
200 chosen to ensure the limit never gets so small that the ledger becomes
201 unusable. The "target" value of 50 was chosen so the limit never gets large
202 enough to invite abuse, but keeps up if the network stays healthy and
203 active. These exact values were chosen experimentally, and can easily
204 change in the future.
205 * *Expected ledger size growth and reduction percentages*. The growth
206 value of 20% was chosen to allow the limit to grow quickly as load
207 increases, but not so quickly as to allow bad actors to run unrestricted.
208 The reduction value of 50% was chosen to cause the limit to drop
209 significantly, but not so drastically that the limit cannot quickly
210 recover if the problem is temporary. These exact values were chosen
211 experimentally, and can easily change in the future.
212 * *Minimum `lastLedgerMedianFeeLevel`*. The value of 500 was chosen to
213 ensure that the first escalated fee was more significant and noticable
214 than what the default would allow. This exact value was chosen
215 experimentally, and can easily change in the future.
216 * *Transaction queue size limit*. The limit is computed based on the
217 base fee transaction limit per ledger, so that the queue can grow
218 automatically as the ripple network's performance improves, allowing
219 more transactions per second, and thus more transactions per ledger
220 to process successfully. The limit of 20 ledgers was used to provide
221 a balance between resource (specifically memory) usage, and giving
222 transactions a realistic chance to be processed. The minimum size of
223 2000 transactions was chosen to allow a decent functional backlog during
224 network congestion conditions. These exact values were
225 chosen experimentally, and can easily change in the future.
226 * *Maximum retries*. A transaction in the queue can attempt to apply
227 to the open ledger, but get a retry (`ter`) code up to 10 times, at
228 which point, it will be removed from the queue and dropped. The
229 value was chosen to be large enough to allow temporary failures to clear
230 up, but small enough that the queue doesn't fill up with stale
231 transactions which prevent lower fee level, but more likely to succeed,
232 transactions from queuing.
233 * *Maximum transactions per account*. A single account can have up to 10
234 transactions in the queue at any given time. This is primarily to
235 mitigate the lost cost of broadcasting multiple transactions if one of
236 the earlier ones fails or is otherwise removed from the queue without
237 being applied to the open ledger. The value was chosen arbitrarily, and
238 can easily change in the future.
239 * *Minimum last ledger sequence buffer*. If a transaction has a
240 `LastLedgerSequence` value, and cannot be processed into the open
241 ledger, that `LastLedgerSequence` must be at least 2 more than the
242 sequence number of the open ledger to be considered for the queue. The
243 value was chosen to provide a balance between letting the user control
244 the lifespan of the transaction, and giving a queued transaction a
245 chance to get processed out of the queue before getting discarded,
246 particularly since it may have dependent transactions also in the queue,
247 which will never succeed if this one is discarded.
248 * *Replaced transaction fee increase*. Any transaction in the queue can be
249 replaced by another transaction with the same sequence number and at
250 least a 25% higher fee level. The 25% increase is intended to cover the
251 resource cost incurred by broadcasting the original transaction to the
252 network. This value was chosen experimentally, and can easily change in
253 the future.

255 ### `fee` command
256 
257 **The `fee` RPC and WebSocket command is still experimental, and may
258 change without warning.**
259 
260 `fee` takes no parameters, and returns information about the current local
261 [fee escalation](#fee-escalation) and [transaction queue](#transaction-queue)
262 state as both fee levels and drops. The drop values assume a
263 single-singed reference transaction. It is up to the user to compute the
264 neccessary fees for other types of transactions. (E.g. multiply all drop
265 values by 5 for a multi-signed transaction with 4 signatures.)
266 
267 The `fee` result is always instantanteous, and relates to the open
268 ledger. It includes the sequence number of the current open ledger,
269 but may not make sense if rippled is not synced to the network.
270 
271 Result format:
272 ```
273 {
274  "result" : {
275  "current_ledger_size" : "16", // number of transactions in the open ledger
276  "current_queue_size" : "2", // number of transactions waiting in the queue
277  "expected_ledger_size" : "15", // one less than the number of transactions that can get into the open ledger for the base fee.
278  "max_queue_size" : "300", // number of transactions allowed into the queue
279  "ledger_current_index" : 123456789, // sequence number of the current open ledger
280  "levels" : {
281  "reference_level" : "256", // level of a reference transaction. Always 256.
282  "minimum_level" : "256", // minimum fee level to get into the queue. If >256, indicates the queue is full.
283  "median_level" : "281600", // lastLedgerMedianFeeLevel used in escalation calculations.
284  "open_ledger_level" : "320398" // minimum fee level to get into the open ledger immediately.
285  },
286  "drops" : {
287  "base_fee" : "10", // base fee of a reference transaction in drops.
288  "minimum_fee" : "10", // minimum drops to get a reference transaction into the queue. If >base_fee, indicates the queue is full.
289  "median_fee" : "11000", // drop equivalent of "median_level" for a reference transaction.
290  "open_ledger_fee" : "12516" // minimum drops to get a reference transaction into the open ledger immediately.
291  }
292  }
293 }
294 ```

296 ### [`server_info`](https://ripple.com/build/rippled-apis/#server-info) command
297 
298 **The fields listed here are still experimental, and may change
299 without warning.**
300 
301 Up to two fields in `server_info` output are related to fee escalation.
302 
303 1. `load_factor_fee_escalation`: The factor on base transaction cost
304 that a transaction must pay to get into the open ledger. This value can
305 change quickly as transactions are processed from the network and
306 ledgers are closed. If not escalated, the value is 1, so will not be
307 returned.
308 2. `load_factor_fee_queue`: If the queue is full, this is the factor on
309 base transaction cost that a transaction must pay to get into the queue.
310 If not full, the value is 1, so will not be returned.
311 
312 In all cases, the transaction fee must be high enough to overcome both
313 `load_factor_fee_queue` and `load_factor` to be considered. It does not
314 need to overcome `load_factor_fee_escalation`, though if it does not, it
315 is more likely to be queued than immediately processed into the open
316 ledger.
317 
318 ### [`server_state`](https://ripple.com/build/rippled-apis/#server-state) command
319 
320 **The fields listed here are still experimental, and may change
321 without warning.**
322 
323 Three fields in `server_state` output are related to fee escalation.
324 
325 1. `load_factor_fee_escalation`: The factor on base transaction cost
326 that a transaction must pay to get into the open ledger. This value can
327 change quickly as transactions are processed from the network and
328 ledgers are closed. The ratio between this value and
329 `load_factor_fee_reference` determines the multiplier for transaction
330 fees to get into the current open ledger.
331 2. `load_factor_fee_queue`: This is the factor on base transaction cost
332 that a transaction must pay to get into the queue. The ratio between
333 this value and `load_factor_fee_reference` determines the multiplier for
334 transaction fees to get into the transaction queue to be considered for
335 a later ledger.
336 3. `load_factor_fee_reference`: Like `load_base`, this is the baseline
337 that is used to scale fee escalation computations.
338 
339 In all cases, the transaction fee must be high enough to overcome both
340 `load_factor_fee_queue` and `load_factor` to be considered. It does not
341 need to overcome `load_factor_fee_escalation`, though if it does not, it
342 is more likely to be queued than immediately processed into the open
343 ledger.
